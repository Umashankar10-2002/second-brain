# Video Ideas and Packaging Anxiety

Date: 2026-05-09

## Original Journal Entry

I often struggle to think of ideas for new videos. Actually, that's not true. I'll have a ton of ideas for videos but decide not to make them because I'm worried they won't get a good view count. I have a lot of ideas for videos that I think would be fun to make or helpful to some people but never end up making it because I know it won't be a hit. I worry that a few videos that underperform will make it so future video also underperform and then send me in a downward spiral. I also struggle with packaging. I know how to make titles that are clickbaity and will make the videos perform better but I don't like doing that. So I'll use more literal titles that I know will get less engagement but are also more accurate to the video. I wrestle with this constantly.

## Response

The pattern here is not an idea problem. It is a publishing-risk problem disguised as an idea problem.

You are filtering ideas through a single question: "Will this be a hit?" That question is too heavy for the idea stage. It turns every possible video into a referendum on the channel's future, which makes even good ideas feel dangerous.

The wiki has a useful contrast. Grox's villager manhunt source is packaged around an instantly legible premise: beat Minecraft while one million villagers hunt him ([[wiki/1,000,000 Villager MANHUNT]]). Technoblade's refrigerator video is similarly clear: win a MrBeast contest for a ridiculous prize ([[wiki/I Must Win MrBeast's $10,000 Refrigerator]]). ProfessorBiggy's MrBeast speedrun parody is also easy to grasp before watching ([[wiki/If MrBeast did a Minecraft Speedrun]]). These are not merely clickbait titles. They are high-friction ideas compressed into simple promises.

The move is not "be more clickbaity." The move is to separate accuracy from literalness. A title can be accurate without being flat. A package can be vivid without lying.

## Useful Reframe

Use two different standards:

- Greenlight standard: "Is this worth making for the audience, the channel, or my own taste?"
- Packaging standard: "Can I express the strongest honest promise of this video in one sentence?"

Do not require every greenlit video to look like a hit. That will kill the channel's creative range. Instead, decide in advance what role each video plays.

## Suggested Video Portfolio

Use a simple mix:

| Type | Purpose | Risk |
|---|---|---|
| Tentpole | Designed to perform strongly | High effort, high packaging pressure |
| Useful | Helps a specific audience deeply | Medium performance, high trust |
| Fun | Keeps taste and energy alive | Variable performance, high creative value |
| Experiment | Tests a title, format, topic, or premise | Expected to be noisy |

If a video underperforms, it should not mean "the channel is doomed." It should mean "what type was this, and what did it teach?"

## Packaging Rule

For every video idea, write three titles:

1. Literal title: the plain accurate version.
2. Strong honest title: the clearest compelling promise.
3. Too-far title: the clickbait version you would not use.

Then publish from category 2.

This gives you a way to use your packaging skill without betraying your taste.

## Practical Tactic

Create a "protected batch" of 5 videos where the goal is not views. The goal is output and learning. Before posting them, decide:

- what each video is testing
- what would count as a useful result besides views
- what title/thumbnail promise is honest but not timid

This prevents one underperforming upload from becoming a story about your future.

## Follow-Up Prompts

- What are 10 video ideas you are avoiding because they might not be hits?
- Which of those are "useful" videos rather than "tentpole" videos?
- For one idea, what is the literal title, the honest strong title, and the too-far title?
- What would you make if the next 5 uploads were not allowed to affect your self-trust?
