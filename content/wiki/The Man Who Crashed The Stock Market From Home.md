# The Man Who Crashed The Stock Market From Home

## Summary

Hamish Hodder explains the 2010 Flash Crash through the story of Navinder Singh Sarao, a trader operating from his home in West London. The video argues that the crash exposed fragile market structure, high-frequency trading dynamics, spoofing, automated sell-order design, and the difficulty of interpreting liquidity in highly automated markets.

The narrative follows Sarao's trading background, the mechanics of spoofing and layering, the May 6, 2010 crash, the role of a large mutual-fund sell order, and the later legal and regulatory consequences. The source presents Sarao as a major aggravating factor, while also noting that broader system design failures mattered.

## Key Points

- The Flash Crash rapidly erased and then recovered enormous market value.
- Sarao used spoofing-style tactics to place and cancel misleading orders.
- High-frequency trading amplified instability by creating high volume without durable liquidity.
- A large automated sell order helped trigger the cascade.
- The aftermath contributed to regulatory attention on spoofing, circuit breakers, and automated market structure.
